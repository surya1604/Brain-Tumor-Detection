WHAT IS BRAIN TUMOR?

A brain tumor is an abnormal growth of cells in the brain. These tumors can be either benign (non-cancerous) or malignant (cancerous). They can develop from brain cells, or they can spread from other parts of the body to the brain (known as metastatic tumors). Brain tumors can interfere with normal brain function and may cause a variety of symptoms depending on their location and size, including headaches, seizures, changes in vision, and cognitive impairment. Treatment options for brain tumors include surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, and targeted drug therapy.

TYPES

  1. Glioma: These tumors originate from the glial cells, which are supportive cells in the brain. Gliomas include astrocytomas, oligodendrogliomas, and ependymomas.
  2. Meningioma: These tumors develop in the meninges, the membranes that cover the brain and spinal cord.
  3. Pituitary: These tumors form in the pituitary gland, which is located at the base of the brain and regulates hormone production.
  4. Metastatic: These tumors originate from cancer cells that have spread (metastasized) to the brain from other parts of the body, such as the lungs, breast, or colon.

SYMPTOMS

  • Headaches, which may worsen over time and become more frequent
  • Nausea or vomiting, especially in the morning
  • Changes in vision, such as blurred or double vision
  • Difficulty speaking or understanding language
  • Weakness or numbness in the arms or legs
  • Seizures
  • Changes in personality or behavior
  • Difficulty with balance or coordination
  • Memory problems
  • Increased pressure inside the skull, leading to symptoms such as drowsiness or confusion
